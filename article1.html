Are We the Most Connected or the Most Isolated Generation?

Home

Written by MERCIER Alexis, On October 29, 2025

From mornings alarms to late night scrolling, smartphones are everywhere. Indeed, 97% of young adults (18-29) in the USA own a smartphone, according to the Pew Research Center. This device has become way more than a tool, its a new landscape of communication. We can communicate with people all around the world at any time.

This constant global connection also brings a serious problem. Even though young adults are more connected than ever, many of them say they feel increasingly lonely and stressed. Studies from Europe and North America show that today’s young people are, in fact, the loneliest generation. This suggests that online communication often doesn’t provide the real sense of belonging that people need.

A recent study conducted by the Joint Research Centre of the European Commission (2024) shows that 34.5% of people aged 16 to 30 use social networking sites for more than two hours per day, and 26.1% use instant messaging tools for the same amount of time. Among people aged 31 and older, these numbers drop to 13.1% and 8.8%, respectively. The report also reveals that over one third of young respondents show signs of social media addiction, compared with about 12% of older adults.

The study found that spending more than two hours per day on social networks is linked to a significant increase in feelings of loneliness. Heavy passive use such has scrolling without interacting is especially connected to isolation.

Other studies reinforce these findings. In France, the Fondation de France / Crédoc Barometer of Loneliness (2021) shows that 21% of 15 to 30 year-olds experience social isolation, a number that has risen sharply in recent years. Similarly, the DREES reports that depressive symptoms among 15 to 24 year-olds nearly doubled during the first COVID-19 lockdown. These trends suggest that hyperconnection alone cannot guarantee emotional support and may even contribute to stress and anxiety when online interactions replace face-to-face contact.

Several factors contribute to this paradox. Social media often encourages constant comparison, which can harm self-esteem. Heavy academic or work pressure, and the ongoing effects of the pandemic reduce opportunities for face-to-face interactions. In addition, spending so much time online can affect sleep and physical activity, which also impacts mental health.

Fortunately, there are initiatives and strategies to help reduce this sense of isolation. Some young people visit “no Wi-Fi cafés” to encourage conversation without digital distractions. Others join offline discussion groups, sports clubs, or hobby communities, which provide real social support. Some apps now focus on building friendships and support networks, rather than just dating or social media popularity. Experts also recommend setting limits on screen time, practicing mindfulness, and engaging in regular physical activity to maintain mental well-being.

This creates a clear paradox. Young people today are more connected than any previous generation, yet many still feel lonely and isolated. Technology can bring people together, but it cannot replace genuine human interaction. The challenge for this generation is to find a balance between digital and real-life connections, using technology as a tool for meaningful communication rather than a substitute for it.
